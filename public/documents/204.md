# Proposition
What are the singular points of the following differential equations?

a) $x^2 \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} - (8 - x^3) \frac{dy}{dx} + (1-9x) y = 0$

b) $(x-9) \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} - x^3 \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{x^2} y = 0$

c) $x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + x^3\frac{dy}{dx} + x^2 y = 0$

d) $(x^2 + 3x + 2) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + \frac{dy}{dx} + y = 0$

# Step-by-step

$$P(x) y'' + Q(x) y' + R(x) y = 0$$
$$\frac{P(x)}{P(x)} y'' + \frac{Q(x)}{P(x)} y' + \frac{R(x)}{P(x)} y = 0$$
$$y'' + p(x) y' + q(x) y = 0$$

# Answers
a) $x = 0$

b) $x_1 = 9$ e $x_2 = 0$

c) $x = \infty$

d) $x_1 = -1$ e $x_2 = -2$

**Observation:** If an equation has absolutely no singular points in the finite plane—like equation (c)—it is mathematically guaranteed that the singularity is hiding at infinity. However, in all of the alternatives above, there are also singular points at $\infty$, but they were omitted by the author. A lack of consistency in the textbook to not let (c) empty.
