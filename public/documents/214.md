# Proposition
Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of A:

a) $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$
b) $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 4 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$

Solved items:

- a
- b
- a

# Step-by-step
Observation: 121.md treats a similar problem.

## General
**Eigenvalue equation**
$$A \vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v}$$

**Characteristic Equation (finds the scalar $\lambda$)**
$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$

**Homogeneous Equation (finds the eigenvectors $\vec{v}$)**
$$(A - \lambda_i I) \vec{v_i} = \vec{0}$$

**Sum of Eigenvalues**
$$\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i = \text{tr}(A)$$

**Product of Eigenvalues**
$$\prod_{i=1}^n \lambda_i = \det(A)$$

**Diagonalization**
$$D = P^{-1} A P$$

## a
$$\boxed{det (A - \lambda I) = 0}$$

$$A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \cr 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} - \lambda \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \cr 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \cr 1 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

$$[(2-\lambda)(2-\lambda)] - [(1)(1)] = 0$$

$$4 - 2\lambda - 2\lambda + \lambda^2 - 1 = 0$$

$$\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$$

$$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = (-4)^2 - 4 \cdot 1 \cdot 3 = 4$$

$$\lambda_1, \lambda_2 = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a} = \frac{-(-4) \pm \sqrt{4}}{2 \cdot 1} = \frac{4 \pm 2}{2} = 3, 1$$

---

$$\boxed{(A - \lambda_i I) \vec{v}_i = \vec{0}}$$

$$\lambda_1 : \begin{pmatrix} 2-3 & 1 \cr 1 & 2-3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \cr v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \vec{0}$$

$$\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \cr 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \cr v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -v_1 + v_2 \cr v_1 - v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \cr 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\vec{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \cr 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\lambda_2 : \begin{pmatrix} 2-1 & 1 \cr 1 & 2-1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \cr v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \vec{0}$$

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \cr 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \cr v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 + v_2 \cr v_1 + v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \cr 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\vec{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \cr 1 \end{pmatrix}$$








# Answers
a) $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = 3$, $\vec{v_1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$, $\vec{v_2} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$

b) $\lambda_1 = 2$, $\lambda_2 = -1$, $\vec{v_1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$, $\vec{v_2} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$
