# Proposition
Solve for y in these differential equations, use the method of integrating factors:

a) $\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 4$, where $y(0) = 3$
b) $\frac{dy}{dx} + 3y = 9$, where $y(0) = 8$
c) $\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 14$, where $y(0) = 9$
d) $\frac{dy}{dx} + 9y = 63$, where $y(0) = 8$

Solved items: 
- a, b
- c, d
- a, b
- c
- d
- a


# Step-by-step

## a - Method of Integrating Factors through the general form
The general form of a first-order linear ordinary differential equation is:
$$
y' + P(x) y = Q(x)
$$
The solution is given by:
$$
y = \frac{1}{\mu(x)} \left( \int \mu(x) Q(x) \, dx + C \right)
$$
where the integrating factor $\mu(x)$ is defined as:
$$
\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) \, dx}
$$

---

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 4, \quad y(0) = 3
$$
$$
y' + P(x)y = Q(x) \quad y = \frac{1}{\mu (x)} \left(\int \mu (x) Q(x) dx + C \right) \quad \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) \, dx}
$$
$$
P(x) = 2 \quad Q(x) = 4
$$
$$
\mu(x) = e^{\int 2 \, dx} = e^{2x}
$$
$$
y = \frac{1}{e^{2x}} \left(\int e^{2x} 4 \, dx + C\right)
$$
$$
= \frac{1}{e^{2x}} \left(2 e^{2x} + C\right) = 2 + Ce^{-2x}
$$
$$
y(0) = 3 \implies y = 2 + e^{-2x}
$$


## a - Method of Integrating Factors from first principles

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 4
$$
$$
{\color{red} \mu(x) \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu(x) 2y} = \mu(x) 4
$$
$$
{\color{green} \mu(x) \frac{dy}{dx}} + {\color{blue}\mu(x) 2y} = \mu(x) 4
$$

---

$$
\frac{d}{dx} fg = f g' + f' g
$$
$$
\frac{d}{dx} \mu(x) y = \mu(x) y' + \mu'(x) y
$$
$$
{\color{red} \frac{d}{dx} \mu(x) y} = {\color{green}\mu(x) \frac{dy}{dx}} + {\color{blue}\frac{d\mu(x)}{dx} y}
$$

---

$$
{\color{blue}\frac{d\mu(x)}{dx} y} = {\color{blue}\mu(x) 2y}
$$
$$
\frac{d\mu(x)}{dx} \cancel{y} = \mu(x) 2\cancel{y}
$$
$$
\frac{d\mu(x)}{\mu(x)} = 2 dx
$$
$$
\int \frac{d\mu(x)}{\mu(x)} = \int 2 dx
$$
$$
\ln |\mu(x)| + C_1 = 2x + C_2
$$
$$
\ln |\mu(x)| = 2x + C_3
$$
$$
e^{\ln |\mu(x)|} = e^{2x + C_3}
$$
$$
\mu(x) = e^{2x} e^{C_3} = C_4 e^{2x}
$$

---

$$
{\color{red} \frac{d}{dx} \mu(x) y} = \mu(x) 4
$$
$$
\frac{d}{dx} C_4 e^{2x} y = C_4 e^{2x} 4
$$
$$
\frac{d}{dx} \cancel{C_4} e^{2x} y = \cancel{C_4} e^{2x} 4
$$
$$
\frac{d}{dx} (e^{2x} y) = (e^{2x} 4)
$$
$$
d(e^{2x} y) = (e^{2x} 4) dx
$$
$$
\int d(e^{2x} y) = \int (e^{2x} 4) dx
$$
$$
e^{2x} y = {\color{purple} \int (e^{2x} 4) dx}
$$

---

$$
\int (e^{2x} 4) dx = 4 \int (e^{2x}) dx
$$
$$
u = 2x \implies \frac{du}{dx} = 2 \implies dx = \frac{1}{2} du
$$
$$
4 \int e^u \frac{1}{2} du = 2 \int e^u du = 2 e^u + C_5 = {\color{purple} 2 e^{2x} + C_5}
$$

---

$$
e^{2x} y = {\color{purple} 2 e^{2x} + C_5}
$$
$$
\frac{e^{2x} y}{e^{2x}} = \frac{2 e^{2x}}{e^{2x}} + \frac{C_5}{e^{2x}}
$$
$$
\frac{\cancel{e^{2x}} y}{\cancel{e^{2x}}} = \frac{2 \cancel{e^{2x}}}{\cancel{e^{2x}}} + \frac{C_5}{e^{2x}}
$$
$$
y = 2 + C_5 e^{-2x}
$$

---

$$
y(0) = 3
$$
$$
y(0) = 2 + C_5 e^{-2(0)} = 2 + C_5 e^{0} = 2 + C_5 = 3
$$
$$
C_5 = 1
$$

$$
\boxed{y = 2 + e^{-2x}}
$$




# Answer
a) $y = 2 + e^{-2x}$
b) $y = 3 + 5e^{-3x}$
c) $y = 7 + 2e^{-2x}$
d) $y = 7 + e^{-9x}$
